# The Taste Bench — Methodology Paper

**v0.1 — March 2026**
**Authors:** Tijs Nieuwboer

---

## Abstract

The Taste Bench is a systematic evaluation framework for measuring digital taste — the aesthetic, intellectual, and communicative quality of a person's public online presence. Using AI-powered deep analysis across multiple platforms, we score individuals on six core dimensions and produce a composite Taste Score (0–100). This paper describes our methodology, scoring rubrics, data collection approach, and the philosophy behind quantifying something inherently subjective.

---

## 1. Introduction

### What is Taste?

Taste is the invisible hand behind every choice you make in public. It's not about what you like — it's about *how* you like it, *why* you like it, and whether those choices form a coherent identity.

In the digital age, taste is expressed through:
- What you post and what you don't
- What you share, retweet, and amplify
- How you write — word choice, economy, tone
- Your visual aesthetic — profile design, website, portfolio
- Your references — who and what you cite
- Your opinions — what you stand for and what you avoid

Everyone has taste. Most people's is unconscious. The Taste Bench makes it visible.

### Why Measure Taste?

Taste is the last unquantified human quality. We measure IQ, EQ, credit scores, follower counts, engagement rates — but nobody has tried to systematically evaluate the *quality* of someone's digital presence.

Until now.

The Taste Bench exists because:
1. **Self-awareness is valuable** — knowing your taste profile helps you improve
2. **Taste is a signal** — in a world of infinite content, taste is the filter
3. **It's fun** — getting roasted by an AI judge is inherently shareable
4. **Benchmarks work** — what gets measured gets improved

## 2. The Six Dimensions of Taste

We evaluate taste across six orthogonal dimensions. Each captures a different aspect of digital presence quality.

### 2.1 References (0–100)
**What it measures:** The quality and originality of your intellectual inputs.

Do you cite interesting, obscure, or insightful sources? Or do you only engage with whatever's trending? References reveal your information diet — and your information diet reveals your mind.

**High scorers:** Cite primary sources, share niche finds, have eclectic reading lists, reference ideas across domains.

**Low scorers:** Only share viral content, retweet trending takes, reference the same 5 thought leaders everyone else does.

**Scoring rubric:**
- 90-100: Consistently surfaces things others haven't seen. Cross-domain references. Primary sources.
- 70-89: Good mix of mainstream and niche. Occasionally surprising.
- 50-69: Mostly mainstream but shows some independent discovery.
- 30-49: Algorithm-driven consumption. Predictable references.
- 0-29: Exclusively trending content. Zero original discovery.

### 2.2 Originality (0–100)
**What it measures:** Are you creating the wave or riding it?

Originality isn't about being weird for the sake of it. It's about having a perspective that's genuinely yours — not a remix of someone else's take.

**High scorers:** Recognizable voice, unique framing of ideas, create formats others copy, ahead of trends.

**Low scorers:** Repost templates, use trending audio/formats, opinions indistinguishable from their peer group.

**Scoring rubric:**
- 90-100: Genuinely creates new ideas, formats, or perspectives. Unmistakable voice.
- 70-89: Strong personal voice with occasional truly original takes.
- 50-69: Has a voice but operates within established frameworks.
- 30-49: Follows trends with personal flavor. Hard to distinguish from peers.
- 0-29: Copy-paste culture. Could be anyone.

### 2.3 Consistency (0–100)
**What it measures:** Does your taste hold across platforms and contexts?

The most tasteful people are the same everywhere. Their Twitter, LinkedIn, website, and real life feel like the same person. Inconsistency reveals performance — consistency reveals authenticity.

**High scorers:** Unified aesthetic and voice across all platforms. Recognizable regardless of medium.

**Low scorers:** Corporate on LinkedIn, edgy on Twitter, generic website. Different person on each platform.

**Scoring rubric:**
- 90-100: Perfect cross-platform coherence. Same person everywhere.
- 70-89: Mostly consistent with appropriate platform adaptation (not fragmentation).
- 50-69: Some inconsistencies but core identity is traceable.
- 30-49: Noticeably different personas across platforms.
- 0-29: Completely fragmented identity. Platform-dependent personality.

### 2.4 Communication (0–100)
**What it measures:** Economy of language, clarity, and signal-to-noise ratio.

Great communicators say more with less. They don't hedge, they don't over-explain, they don't fill space with filler. Every word earns its place.

**High scorers:** Concise, clear, memorable phrasing. High signal-to-noise. Know when to speak and when to stay quiet.

**Low scorers:** Verbose, hedging, buzzword-heavy, over-explain, thread-bro energy.

**Scoring rubric:**
- 90-100: Every post is tight. Memorable phrasing. Says what others can't articulate.
- 70-89: Generally clear and well-written. Occasional filler.
- 50-69: Competent but unremarkable. Gets the point across.
- 30-49: Verbose or unclear. Buzzwords. Corporate speak.
- 0-29: Noise. Word salad. "Thrilled to announce" energy.

### 2.5 Courage (0–100)
**What it measures:** Do you have actual opinions or just safe takes?

The internet rewards consensus and punishes dissent. Courage is having a genuine position that risks disagreement. It's not about being contrarian — it's about being honest.

**High scorers:** Take real positions, disagree publicly when it matters, have unpopular opinions they can defend, aren't afraid of engagement.

**Low scorers:** Only positive takes, never disagree, "great thread!" energy, opinions perfectly aligned with their peer group's consensus.

**Scoring rubric:**
- 90-100: Genuinely controversial positions, well-defended. Intellectual courage.
- 70-89: Takes real positions but picks battles wisely.
- 50-69: Has opinions but stays within safe boundaries.
- 30-49: Crowd-pleasing. Avoids anything that might get pushback.
- 0-29: Zero edge. Pure consensus. "I love this community" energy.

### 2.6 Self-Awareness (0–100)
**What it measures:** Do you know what you are?

Self-awareness is the meta-dimension. It's knowing your strengths, acknowledging your limitations, and not performing a version of yourself you're not. The gap between who you are and who you pretend to be is the self-awareness gap.

**High scorers:** Honest about what they are and aren't. Self-deprecating without being insecure. Know their lane.

**Low scorers:** LinkedIn "thought leaders" who've never led anything. "Serial entrepreneurs" with no exits. Main character syndrome without the plot.

**Scoring rubric:**
- 90-100: Perfectly calibrated self-image. Knows exactly what they are.
- 70-89: Generally self-aware with occasional blind spots.
- 50-69: Some mismatch between self-image and reality.
- 30-49: Significant delusion in one or more areas.
- 0-29: Complete disconnect. Performance ≠ reality.

## 3. Composite Score Calculation

The composite Taste Score is a weighted average of all six dimensions:

| Dimension | Weight | Rationale |
|-----------|--------|-----------|
| References | 15% | Input quality matters but isn't everything |
| Originality | 20% | The core of taste — are you creating or copying? |
| Consistency | 15% | Authenticity signal |
| Communication | 20% | How you express taste matters as much as having it |
| Courage | 15% | Taste without conviction is just aesthetics |
| Self-Awareness | 15% | The meta-skill that amplifies everything else |

**Formula:**
```
TasteScore = (References × 0.15) + (Originality × 0.20) + (Consistency × 0.15) + 
             (Communication × 0.20) + (Courage × 0.15) + (Self-Awareness × 0.15)
```

### Score Tiers

| Score | Tier | Description |
|-------|------|-------------|
| 90-100 | **Legendary** | Genuine tastemaker. Sets the standard. |
| 80-89 | **Exceptional** | Strong taste with few weaknesses. |
| 70-79 | **Tasteful** | Above average with room to sharpen. |
| 60-69 | **Developing** | Shows promise but inconsistent. |
| 50-59 | **Mid** | Average. Neither impressive nor offensive. |
| 40-49 | **Basic** | Following trends, not setting them. |
| 30-39 | **Struggling** | Significant taste gaps across dimensions. |
| 0-29 | **Tasteless** | Algorithmically generated persona. |

## 4. Data Collection

### 4.1 Sources
We collect data from up to four sources per evaluation:

1. **X/Twitter** — Profile bio, recent tweets (50), retweets, likes, engagement patterns, profile aesthetic
2. **LinkedIn** — Headline, about section, experience, posts, recommendations
3. **Website/Portfolio** — Visual design, copy, content choices, what they choose to show
4. **Self-Description** — Optional free-text input (also evaluated for self-awareness)

### 4.2 Scraping Methodology
- Twitter/X data collected via Apify actors (profile + last 50 tweets)
- LinkedIn data collected via Apify actors (public profile)
- Websites scraped for text content and visual screenshots
- All URLs screenshotted for visual analysis evidence

### 4.3 Privacy & Ethics
- We only analyze **publicly available** information
- No private messages, locked accounts, or non-public data
- Users submit themselves voluntarily
- Scores are public by design (this is a benchmark)
- No personal data stored beyond what's publicly available

## 5. AI Judge: Architecture

### 5.1 Model
We use Claude (Anthropic) as the primary judge. Claude was chosen for:
- Nuanced understanding of cultural context
- Ability to analyze writing style and patterns
- Strong reasoning about subjective qualities
- Honest and direct communication style

### 5.2 Analysis Process
1. **Data Assembly** — All scraped data compiled into a structured context document
2. **Cross-Platform Analysis** — Claude compares presence across platforms for consistency
3. **Pattern Recognition** — Identifies themes, influences, aesthetic choices, communication patterns
4. **Dimensional Scoring** — Each dimension scored independently with specific justification
5. **Composite Calculation** — Weighted average computed
6. **Verdict Generation** — Witty title + detailed analysis + recommendations

### 5.3 Reproducibility
Same input data will produce similar (±5 points) but not identical scores on re-evaluation. This reflects the inherent subjectivity of taste evaluation. We report this as a feature, not a bug — even human judges would show similar variance.

### 5.4 Bias Considerations
- English-language bias in communication scoring
- Western-centric aesthetic norms in some dimensions
- Tech/creative industry skew in reference scoring
- We acknowledge these limitations and plan to expand cultural context in future versions

## 6. Limitations & Future Work

### Current Limitations
- Single-point-in-time evaluation (taste evolves)
- Text-heavy analysis (limited visual/aesthetic scoring currently)
- English-language primary
- AI judge subjectivity (consistent but not objective)

### Planned Improvements
- **Longitudinal tracking** — Re-evaluate over time to track taste evolution
- **Comparative analytics** — "You score higher than X% in Originality"
- **Visual analysis** — Actual screenshot analysis for aesthetic scoring via vision models
- **Multi-model judging panel** — Multiple AI judges for more robust scoring
- **Community calibration** — Human judges validate AI scores to reduce bias
- **Industry-specific benchmarks** — Taste norms differ by field
- **Taste DNA report** — Detailed influence mapping and recommendation engine

---

## 7. Conclusion

Taste has been the last unmeasured quality of online presence. The Taste Bench changes that by providing a systematic, transparent, and entertaining evaluation framework.

We don't claim to be objective — taste is inherently subjective. But we claim to be *consistent*, *insightful*, and *useful*. The goal isn't to rank humans like LLMs. It's to hold up a mirror and say: "This is what your digital presence says about you. Is that what you intended?"

The Taste Bench: *How good is your taste, really?*
