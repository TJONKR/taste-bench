## 1. What Is Taste? The Philosophical Foundations

### 1.1 Ancient Roots

The Greeks had no word for "taste" in our sense. The discipline of aesthetics only emerged in 18th-century Germany. But the foundations were there.

**Plato** believed beauty is objective — grounded in eternal Forms. An object becomes beautiful by partaking in the Form of Beauty. His "Ladder of Love" (Symposium) describes ascending from loving one beautiful body to grasping "Beauty itself" — eternal, unchanging, transcendent. He was suspicious of art as mimesis (copies of copies).

**Aristotle** gave us something more practical. Beauty has objective elements — **order, symmetry, and definiteness** — but is connected to human engagement. Crucially, he introduced the idea of the **experienced judge**: having knowledge of any art allows one to be a "good judge." This prefigures Hume's "true judge" by two millennia.

**Longinus** developed the theory of the **sublime**: "A lofty passage does not convince the reason... but takes him out of himself." This anticipates Kant by 1,700 years.

### 1.2 David Hume — "Of the Standard of Taste" (1757)

Hume navigates between pure subjectivism and objectivism. He starts subjective:

> "Beauty is no quality in things themselves: It exists merely in the mind which contemplates them; and each mind perceives a different beauty."

But then argues taste CAN be standardized through qualified judges. He defines the **"true judge"** through five qualities:

1. **Strong sense** — sound understanding
2. **Delicate sentiment** — fine-grained perceptual discrimination
3. **Practice** — extensive experience with artworks
4. **Comparison** — broad knowledge across traditions
5. **Freedom from prejudice** — ability to set aside personal bias

> "The joint verdict of such judges, wherever they are to be found, is the true standard of taste and beauty."

**The Sancho's Kinsmen Story:** Hume borrows from Don Quixote — two relatives taste wine from a cask and detect iron and leather. They're mocked. When the cask is emptied, an iron key with a leather thong is found at the bottom. Trained perception detects real qualities that untrained perception misses.

**Limits Hume acknowledges:** Cultural variation is real. Where differences in "internal frame or external situation" are blameless on both sides, "we seek in vain for a standard." On preference among excellent works: "We choose our favourite author as we do our friend, from a conformity of humour and disposition."

**Relevance:** Hume's "true judge" maps onto trained evaluators, calibrated rating systems, or (aspirationally) AI judges. The five qualities describe a training protocol. But the circularity problem haunts us: who certifies the judges?

### 1.3 Immanuel Kant — Critique of Judgment (1790)

Kant occupies the most fascinating middle position. Taste is subjective (grounded in feeling, not concepts) yet **claims universal validity**.

**The Four Moments:**

1. **Disinterestedness** — Aesthetic pleasure doesn't depend on desire for the object. It's distinct from the agreeable (sensory pleasure) and the good (moral/utilitarian satisfaction). "We take pleasure in something because we judge it beautiful, rather than judging it beautiful because we find it pleasurable."

2. **Universality without concepts** — When you say "this is beautiful," you claim everyone *should* agree, yet this universality isn't based on rules or definitions. It's "subjective universality."

3. **Purposiveness without purpose** — Beautiful objects appear to have a designed order without serving any determinable end. "Lawfulness without law."

4. **Necessity** — The judgment involves an "ought" — a claim that everyone should share it, grounded in *sensus communis* (common sense of taste).

**The "free play" mechanism:** When we encounter beauty, imagination and understanding enter harmonious "free play" — mutual reinforcement creating self-contained pleasure without producing a definite concept.

**Beauty and morality:** "Beauty is the 'symbol' of morality." Both are disinterested, involve free conformity to law, and rest on universal principles.

**Relevance:** Kant's "disinterestedness" is the exact opposite of algorithmic recommendation (driven by engagement, clicks, purchases). He'd likely argue algorithmically-driven taste isn't genuine taste. His *sensus communis* suggests a basis for aggregated judgment — but insists it's an a priori principle, not a statistical average. Crowdsourced ratings approximate but don't achieve Kantian universality.

### 1.4 Pierre Bourdieu — Distinction (1979)

Bourdieu is the demolition crew. Taste is NOT personal, innate, or universal. It is a **social product** determined by class position.

> "Taste classifies, and it classifies the classifier. Social subjects, classified by their classifications, distinguish themselves by the distinctions they make, between the beautiful and the ugly, the distinguished and the vulgar, in which their position in the objective classifications is expressed or betrayed."

**Key concepts:**

- **Habitus** — ingrained dispositions shaped by upbringing, education, environment. We experience socially produced preferences as "natural."
- **Cultural capital** — education, intellect, style of speech/dress that act as social currency. Those with high cultural capital set norms for "good taste."
- **The "pure gaze"** (dominant class) vs **"barbarous taste"** (dominated class) — the pure gaze asserts primacy of form over function. "Barbarous taste" expects objects to serve practical purposes. "The detachment of the pure gaze cannot be dissociated from a general disposition towards the world which is the paradoxical product of conditioning by negative economic necessities — a life of ease."
- **Symbolic violence** — the power of the ruling class to define and impose norms of "good taste" upon all of society, disguising class domination as natural aesthetic truth.

> "To see (voir) is a function of knowledge (savoir)."

**Relevance:** Bourdieu is devastatingly relevant to online taste evaluation. Social media taste performance is pure Bourdieu — people signal cultural capital through what they share, like, and critique. "Good taste" in digital culture remains tied to cultural capital: knowing which obscure artists to reference, which films to champion. **Any taste benchmark must reckon with the possibility that it's measuring cultural capital, not some universal quality.**

**The Omnivore complication:** Richard Peterson (1992) showed that cultural elites shifted from "snobs" (only highbrow) to "omnivores" (consuming across the spectrum). But omnivores are selective — they still apply hierarchical judgments. Omnivorousness may be the new form of distinction.

### 1.5 Susan Sontag — "Notes on Camp" (1964)

Camp is a distinct sensibility whose essence is "the love of the unnatural: of artifice and exaggeration." It's "the answer to the problem: how to be a dandy in the age of mass culture."

> "Camp sees everything in quotation marks. It's not a lamp, but a 'lamp'; not a woman, but a 'woman.' To perceive Camp in objects and persons is to understand Being-as-Playing-a-Role."

> "There exists, indeed, a good taste of bad taste."

**Relevance:** Internet irony culture (memes, shitposting, "so bad it's good") is a direct descendant of camp. Algorithmic systems struggle with camp because they can't distinguish sincere engagement from ironic appreciation. Any taste evaluation must account for layers of irony and intentional "bad taste" that is actually sophisticated.

### 1.6 Modern Philosophy of Taste

**Kyle Chayka — *Filterworld* (2024):** Algorithms "prioritize flatness and sameness, the aesthetics that are the most transmissible across digital platforms." Solution: human curation over algorithmic recommendation. "We should talk even more about the things we like, experience them together, and build up our own careful collections."

**Sianne Ngai — *Our Aesthetic Categories* (2012):** The dominant aesthetics of late capitalism aren't the beautiful and the sublime but the **zany, the cute, and the interesting** — minor categories reflecting "hypercommodified, information-saturated, performance-driven conditions."

**Robin James:** "Vibes are how we perceive ourselves the way AI and finance perceive us." She traces the privatization of aesthetic taste through: omnivorousness (1996) → poptimism → algorithmic personalization (2010s) → vibes → AI slop (2020s).

### 1.7 The Key Tensions

| Tension | Positions |
|---------|-----------|
| **Objective vs. Subjective** | Plato (objective) → Hume (refinable sentiment) → Kant (subjective but universal) → Bourdieu (socially constructed) |
| **Individual vs. Social** | Kant (individual claiming universality) vs. Bourdieu (taste as class product) |
| **Innate vs. Cultivated** | Plato (Forms) vs. Hume (practice) vs. Bourdieu (habitus/education) |
| **Disinterested vs. Interested** | Kant (pure) vs. Bourdieu (always serves class interest) |
| **Human vs. Algorithmic** | Chayka (algorithms flatten) vs. James (vibes = perceiving as algorithms perceive us) |

## 2. The Psychology & Neuroscience of Taste

### 2.1 How Taste Forms

Research converges on a **reinforcement-learning framework** where aesthetic values develop through interaction of innate biases and environmental exposure.

**Innate universals exist.** Preferences for symmetry, high contrast, and curvilinear forms appear at birth. Infants prefer consonant over dissonant musical intervals.

**But culture rapidly takes over.** Children show cultural idiosyncrasies by preschool. Three mechanisms drive divergence:
- Motivation variability — internal drive states modulate learning rates
- Environmental/cultural differences — different stimulus environments
- Stochastic learning — random variation in exposure

**Processing Fluency Theory** (Reber, Schwarz & Winkielman, 2004): **The more fluently the brain processes a stimulus, the more positive the aesthetic response.** This elegantly resolves nature vs. nurture — infants prefer consonance because they process it more easily (innate), while adults develop culture-specific fluency through exposure (acquired).

### 2.2 Taste as Pattern Recognition

**The case FOR:**
- Taste demonstrably involves learned statistical regularities — experts have internalized patterns through massive exposure
- A 2026 paper ("Deconstructing Taste") showed consumer aesthetic perceptions could be partially modeled via computer vision + domain patterns + semantic analysis

**The case AGAINST:**
- Designer-informed features proved more predictive than raw computational features
- "Sporty" and "dynamic" had standardized meanings AI could capture, but "classic" and "elegant" produced heterogeneous interpretations that resisted modeling
- Taste is "partially decomposable but ultimately contextual"

**The critical distinction:** AI can model taste within bounded domains where cultural consensus exists, but struggles with abstract, subjective, or culturally contested aesthetic concepts.

### 2.3 The Mere Exposure Effect

Robert Zajonc (1960s): Simply exposing someone to a stimulus — with no reinforcement — makes them rate it more positively. Works even with subliminal exposure.

- Maximum effect at **10-20 presentations**
- After that, liking may **decline** (Berlyne's inverted-U)
- Two factors: "positive habituation" (increases attractiveness) and "tedium" (decreases it)

**Implication:** Cultural immersion shapes taste through repeated exposure. But overexposure kills — trends die when tedium overtakes habituation.

### 2.4 Expertise: Do Experts Have "Better" Taste?

**The famous wine color experiment (Brochet, 2001):** 54 oenology students got two glasses of the same white wine — one dyed red. They overwhelmingly described the dyed wine using red descriptors. **Experienced tasters were MORE influenced by color than non-experts.**

**What expertise actually changes:**
- Conceptual knowledge structures, not raw perception — experts don't have better noses, they have better *frameworks*
- Neuroimaging shows expertise changes brain activation in conceptual analysis areas, not sensory areas
- Experts may describe things based more on linguistic prototypes than actual properties

**Bottom line:** Expertise produces more consistent internal frameworks and richer vocabularies, but these frameworks can also create systematic biases.

### 2.5 Social Contagion of Taste

Two competing models:
1. **Networks as infrastructure, taste as content** — taste flows through pre-existing connections
2. **Taste as durable, networks as fleeting** — shared taste builds the network (Lizardo, 2006)

**Trend adoption (Rogers):** Innovators (2.5%) → Early Adopters (13.5%) → Early Majority (34%) → Late Majority (34%) → Laggards (16%)

**Trickle directions:**
- Trickle-Down (Veblen/Simmel): Top → bottom, then upper classes abandon
- Trickle-Across: Horizontal among similar groups
- Trickle-Up (Field, 1970): Subcultures → mainstream → elite

**Peterson's "Omnivore" thesis:** High-status individuals shifted from "snobs" to "omnivores" — but they're selective. Little tolerance for genres associated with lowest social status. Omnivorousness may be the new distinction strategy.

### 2.6 The Paradox: Can Taste Be Both Subjective AND Measurable?

**Kant's antinomy:** Beauty is subjective feeling, yet claims everyone should agree.

**Hume's resolution:** Standards emerge through convergence among qualified critics, not objective measurement.

**Bourdieu's demolition:** No genuine good taste — "legitimate taste" is ruling class taste imposed through symbolic violence.

**What empirical research says:**
- A "taste typicality" study (Current Biology, 2022) found people's position on a mainstream-to-unique spectrum is **consistent across sensory modalities** — visual taste correlates with auditory taste. Taste has measurable, stable, cross-modal structure.
- But aesthetic judgments show continuous distributions and enormous individual variation.

**Resolution:** Taste is measurable in its *patterns* and *structures* (who agrees with whom, what predicts preference) while remaining genuinely subjective in its *content*. The paradox is a feature, not a bug.

### 2.7 Taste vs. Preference

| **Preference (hedonic liking)** | **Taste (aesthetic appreciation)** |
|---|---|
| Quick, positive gut reaction | Sustained, reflective engagement |
| "I like this" | "This is good" |
| Requires no justification | Involves evaluation against internalized standards |
| Easy to measure (clicks, engagement) | Resists simple quantification |

For Kant: preference is "the agreeable" (purely subjective); taste is "the beautiful" (claiming universal validity).

### 2.8 Personality and Taste

**Openness to Experience** is the dominant predictor. Correlates positively with emotional aesthetic judgment (the most advanced form) and negatively with concrete judgment (the most superficial).

But: personality barely accounts for individual differences in *content* of taste. It sets disposition toward aesthetic engagement but explains little about what you actually like.

### 2.9 Digital Taste

- Social media is "more about how they present an image themselves to others and not who they really are" — taste as performance and identity construction
- **Aesthetic tribes** (-cores): cottagecore, dark academia, clean girl — community through shared visual languages
- **Liking as taste-making** (Paßmann & Schubert, 2021): Social media "likes" function as taste-making acts, not just preference expression
- **Algorithmic narrowing:** Recommender optimizes for engagement → shows more of what's engaged with → shifts preferences toward what's shown → narrowing spiral of aesthetic exposure

### 2.10 The Neuroscience

**The Aesthetic Triad** (Chatterjee & Vartanian, 2014): Aesthetic experience emerges from three brain systems:
1. **Sensory-motor** — perceptual processing
2. **Emotion-valuation** — hedonic response, reward
3. **Meaning-knowledge** — interpretation, context, memory

**Key fMRI findings:**
- No single "beauty center" in the brain
- **The Default Mode Network surprise:** Intense aesthetic experience activates the DMN — normally active during rest, daydreaming, and self-referential thought. Aesthetic judgment fundamentally involves relating stimuli to the self.
- Beauty activates the same reward circuits as food, sex, and drugs (orbitofrontal cortex, striatum)
- Expertise changes conceptual processing regions, not sensory regions

**Critical insight for the Taste Bench:** Deep aesthetic experience is neurologically self-referential. The most intense judgments involve relating stimuli to one's own identity. This is precisely what AI cannot do from first principles.

## 3. Taste in Tech: The Key Voices

### 3.1 Steve Jobs

**Core thesis:** Taste is bringing culture and humanities into products. It separates products with "spirit" from products that are merely functional.

> "The only problem with Microsoft is they just have no taste... they don't think of original ideas and they don't bring much culture into their product."

> "Ultimately, it comes down to taste. It comes down to trying to expose yourself to the best things that humans have done, and then try to bring those things into what you are doing."

> "When you're a carpenter making a beautiful chest of drawers, you're not going to use a piece of plywood on the back, even though it faces the wall and nobody will ever see it."

> "Your taste gets more refined as you make mistakes... All it takes is a little more time."

Jobs conflated taste with: originality, cultural depth, cross-domain knowledge, craftsmanship in invisible details, and the refusal to compromise.

### 3.2 Rick Rubin

**Core thesis:** Taste is deeply personal, democratic, requires no technical expertise. Everyone has it. The challenge is trusting it.

> "I have no technical ability. And I know nothing about music."

> "The confidence that I have in my taste, and my ability to express what I feel, has proven helpful for artists."

> "Which painting do you like better?... You don't have to know why. Nobody can argue with you."

> "You can't second-guess your own taste for what someone else will like."

Rubin is a "reducer" — getting the point across with the least information possible. He listens "to the feeling."

**The paradox (APA Blog analysis):** Rubin's genius *consists entirely in* his cultivated taste, challenging Kant's separation of genius and taste. His taste IS his technical skill, disguised.

### 3.3 Andrej Karpathy & Vibe Coding

The original tweet (Feb 2, 2025):
> "There's a new kind of coding I call 'vibe coding', where you fully give in to the vibes, embrace exponentials, and forget that the code even exists."

4M+ views. Recognized by Merriam-Webster. The implication: when AI writes code, the human's role is judgment. Taste becomes the primary skill.

By Feb 2026, Karpathy called vibe coding "passe" — replaced by "agentic engineering" with more oversight.

### 3.4 Sam Altman & OpenAI

> "We believe the best research teams are built through context, taste and a real feel for where the field is headed next."

Greg Brockman (Feb 16, 2026): **"Taste is a new core skill."** — 2.6M views, 10K likes. The lightning rod tweet.

### 3.5 Julie Zhuo & Ivan Zhao (Notion)

**Ivan Zhao's three-element framework:**
1. **Capabilities** — skills/knowledge (increasingly AI-supported)
2. **Taste** — values/preferences (vulnerable to AI replication)
3. **Agency** — will/drive (the true human moat)

Zhuo's key insight: The highest form of taste goes beyond prediction to **movement-making** — championing unfamiliar work and convincing others. This may resist AI replication more than mere pattern recognition.

> "In a world where capabilities and even taste becomes commoditized, the will of the hand to act according to our values may well be our most rare and precious resource."

### 3.6 Paul Graham

Resurfaced his 2002 essay "Taste for Makers" on Feb 14, 2026:
> "Prediction: In the AI age, taste will become even more important. When anyone can make anything, the big differentiator is what you choose to make."

> "The recipe for great work is very exacting taste, plus the ability to gratify it."

Graham insists taste is NOT purely subjective: "Your old tastes were not merely different, but worse."

### 3.7 Dieter Rams

"Less, but better." His 10 Principles of Good Design provide the most codifiable taste framework:
- Good design is innovative, useful, aesthetic, understandable, unobtrusive, honest, long-lasting, thorough down to the last detail, environmentally friendly, and involves as little design as possible.

His Braun T3 Pocket Radio inspired the iPod. The iPhone calculator is an homage to his Braun ET44.

### 3.8 Jony Ive

> "What we make testifies who we are. People can sense care and can sense carelessness."

> "Simplicity is not the absence of clutter, that's a consequence of simplicity."

> "'Different' and 'new' is relatively easy. Doing something that's genuinely better is very hard."

At Stripe Sessions 2025: "I think generally in the valley... joy in humans has been missing."

### 3.9 The VC Perspective

**Founder Collective:** "Taste is pattern recognition layered with values — the ability to sense what will resonate, what has staying power." Early-stage investing mirrors art criticism rather than financial analysis.

**Anu Atluru** ("Taste is Eating Silicon Valley," Sept 2024 — the foundational essay): "Software has been commoditized... Taste is the new weapon." "Taste isn't bland — it's opinionated."

**Marc Andreessen:** The core competitiveness of VC lies in "taste" and "networks" — "quite unfair and difficult to replicate."

### 3.10 Ira Glass on the Taste Gap

> "Nobody tells this to people who are beginners. All of us who do creative work, we get into it because we have good taste. But there is this gap. For the first couple years you make stuff, it's just not that good... It is only by going through a volume of work that you will close that gap."

### 3.11 How They All Define Taste

| **Thinker** | **Taste Is...** |
|---|---|
| Steve Jobs | Bringing culture into products; exposure to the best of human achievement |
| Rick Rubin | Confidence in personal preferences; decisive knowledge of like/dislike |
| Karpathy | The judgment layer when execution is automated |
| Sam Altman | Context + feel for direction; strategic judgment |
| Julie Zhuo | Values and preferences; pattern recognition across cultural knowledge |
| Ivan Zhao | Study "sideways through history, not just current tech trends" |
| Paul Graham | Learnable skill; exacting standards + ability to gratify them |
| Dieter Rams | Codifiable principles; "less, but better" |
| Jony Ive | Care made visible; obsessive attention to detail |
| VCs/a16z | Pattern recognition layered with values |

## 4. The 2025-2026 Twitter/X Discourse

### 4.1 The Triggering Moment

**February 14, 2026:** Paul Graham tweets the prediction about taste becoming more important in the AI age.

**February 16, 2026:** Greg Brockman tweets "taste is a new core skill" — 2.6M views, 891 replies, 2.1K retweets, 10K likes. This becomes the lightning rod.

**February 27, 2026:** Sam Altman tells Fortune that taste gets people hired at OpenAI.

### 4.2 The Five Camps

**Camp 1: "Taste is the new moat"** (Graham, Brockman, Altman, Knecht, De Castro, Grigsby)
- Execution is commoditized; taste is what remains scarce and human
- Eric M. De Castro proposes three pillars: historical context ("AI is trapped in the Eternal Now"), constraint/discipline ("taste is defined by what you refuse to do"), and judgment ("the machine can follow the recipe, but only you can taste the sauce")

**Camp 2: "Taste was ALWAYS the moat"** (Aakash Gupta, Jens Axboe, Ben Roy, Shreyas Doshi)
- Nothing new — we had a 30-year window where bad taste could hide behind hard execution
- Aakash Gupta: "AI made execution cheap enough to A/B test judgment directly... you can now see, in real time, the delta between someone with taste directing an agent and someone without taste directing the same agent"

**Camp 3: "Taste won't save you"** (Suhail Gupta, Hilde Johannessen, Jase Cooper, Shrivu Shankar)
- AI is rapidly learning taste; it's alpha, not a moat
- Jase Cooper: "Taste is mostly pattern recognition. AI will be as 'tasteful' as you want it to be"
- Shrivu Shankar: "The question is no longer whether AI can match human taste, but how long until it does in each domain"

**Camp 4: "Against Taste entirely"** (Will Manidis)
- The strongest philosophical critique
- Taste reduces humans to discriminators — in GAN terms, "asking you to be the discriminator" which gets discarded once the generator is good enough
- Taste operates backward — can only select from existing options, cannot generate the genuinely new
- The patron who built the cathedral exercised **will**, not taste
- Alternative: humans should exercise will oriented toward transcendence, not merely curate

**Camp 5: "Taste discourse is gatekeeping"** (Slotnick, anonymous critics, Bourdieu-adjacent)
- "Taste" is nebulous, unassailable, ego-feeding
- Repackages class privilege as a tech skill
- One anonymous X user: "it's almost like these tech guys understand that they don't have good taste so they are trying to turn it into this quantifiable set of data points"
- Reggie James (General Catalyst): memes noting techies would be "ruffled when they learn they're not the top of the taste pyramid"

### 4.3 The "AI Has Statistical Taste" Argument

Multiple voices argue AI produces "median" aesthetic output:
- AI models trained on the internet's average are "engines of conformity that regress to the mean. Taste is the opposite — taste is the anomaly." (De Castro)
- AI-generated websites converge on the same look: purple gradients, rectangular grids, emojis, rounded corners
- Justin Waugh: "the machine doesn't need to understand taste — it just needs to saturate the environment with its output until its version of taste becomes the baseline"
- State of AI in Design Report 2025: 89% of designers say AI improved workflow, but only **58% say it improves quality**

## 5. How Others Have Tried to Measure Taste

### 5.1 The Michelin Guide — Gold Standard

Five criteria, applied identically worldwide:
1. Quality of ingredients
2. Mastery of cooking techniques
3. Harmony of flavours
4. Personality/emotion expressed through dishes
5. Consistency across menu and between visits

**What makes it work:**
- Anonymous inspectors (9-18 months training before working solo)
- Each inspector eats 250+ anonymous meals/year
- Multiple visits across seasons, days, meal times
- Unanimous consensus required for stars
- Annual re-evaluation of all starred restaurants
- Only evaluates food (not ambiance/service) — constraining the subjective space

**The Taste Bench analog:** Trained evaluators, blind assessment, clear criteria, consensus-based scoring, deliberate exclusion of confounding variables.

### 5.2 Wine Scoring — A Cautionary Tale

**Robert Parker's 100-point system:** Most influential, but deeply flawed.

- Parker himself: "The only difference between a 96-, 97-, 98-, 99-, and 100-point wine is really the emotion of the moment"
- In a blind tasting of his "greatest vintage ever" (2005 Bordeaux), Parker couldn't identify any wine correctly
- California State Fair: judges showed significant inconsistencies when identical wines were re-presented
- Non-expert tasters rated expensive wines slightly *lower* in blind tests

**Lesson:** Even in a domain with trained experts and measurable chemical compounds, subjective scoring is poorly reproducible and heavily influenced by context and emotion.

### 5.3 Design Awards

**D&AD Pencils** — Three core questions in order: (1) Is it a brilliant idea? (2) Is it beautifully crafted? (3) Is it fit for purpose? No quotas — some years no Black Pencils. Three-day deliberation.

**Cannes Lions** — Weighted criteria: 40% idea, 40% execution, 20% results. Individual 1-9 scoring → shortlist → calibration → two-thirds majority for medals.

**Pattern:** Multi-stage evaluation with increasing scrutiny, consensus requirements, clear tier definitions.

### 5.4 Music Scoring

**Pitchfork's 0.0-10.0 scale:** 101 points of false precision. Average = 7.0, clustered 6.4-7.8. They acknowledged scores are "an admittedly absurd and subjective metric."

**RateYourMusic:** Sophisticated anti-manipulation weighting. Each user gets a weight (0-100%) based on behavior. Users who rate everything 5 stars or use only extremes are downweighted. One of the few systems that accounts for rater quality.

### 5.5 AI Aesthetic Scoring

**NIMA (Google, 2017):** Predicts the full distribution of human ratings, not binary good/bad. Trained on 255K images, each rated by ~200 people. "Reasonable, though imperfect proxies for human aesthetic judgment."

**AesTest Benchmark (Nov 2025):** First comprehensive benchmark for multimodal LLM aesthetic intelligence. Key finding: largest gap between human professionals and AI appears in **creation** and **photography** tasks (humans: 0.83, best AI: 0.46). Recognizing aesthetic quality is much easier for AI than making taste decisions.

### 5.6 Academic Taste Measurement

**VAST (Visual Aesthetic Sensitivity Test):** Eysenck (1983) — binary choices between non-representational figures. Measures a general factor "T" (for Taste). Proposed expansion to "Aesthetic Quotient" (AQ).

**Taste typicality research:** Shared taste contributes ~50% to face attractiveness ratings but only ~10% to abstract art. Individual taste dominates in complex domains.

### 5.7 Social Metrics as Taste Proxies

Follower count is widely considered a "vanity metric" that does NOT reliably correlate with taste. Engagement rate is better but still measures popularity, not quality. Expertise (not follower count) builds trust.

**Liking as taste-making (Paßmann & Schubert, 2021):** Social media activities are not just taste expression but taste making — they constitute practices of evaluation and distinction.

### 5.8 The Taste Graph

**Hugo Liu's "Taste Fabric" (MIT Media Lab):** Analyzed 100K social network profiles, mapped natural language into taste ontology. Identified four types of taste statements: **prestige** (signaling status), **differentiation** (distinguishing from mainstream), **authenticity** (signaling genuineness), and **theatrical persona** (constructing a character).

### 5.9 The Universal Finding

**Across all domains, the consistent finding: shared/consensus taste is measurable but accounts for a minority of variance; individual taste dominates, is context-dependent, and resists reliable quantification.** The most trusted systems (Michelin, D&AD) succeed not by reducing subjectivity to numbers but by structuring subjective evaluation through trained assessors, clear criteria, multiple evaluations, and consensus.

## 6. Implications for The Taste Bench

### 6.1 What the Research Validates

- **The six dimensions are largely sound** — they map to real aspects of how taste is discussed and evaluated across philosophy, psychology, and tech discourse
- **AI-as-judge is defensible** — Hume's "true judge" framework supports the idea that trained evaluation (even by AI) can approximate taste standards, as long as the criteria are explicit
- **Cross-platform analysis is valuable** — Bourdieu's insights about taste performance across contexts directly supports the Consistency dimension
- **The witty, editorial voice is appropriate** — Sontag's camp sensibility and the entertainment value of taste evaluation are culturally grounded

### 6.2 What the Research Challenges

**The References dimension needs rethinking:**
- Current rubric rewards obscure/niche references highly ("surfaces things others haven't seen")
- But Bourdieu warns: rewarding obscure references may just be measuring cultural capital — access to elite education and environments
- Peterson's omnivore thesis suggests breadth + selectivity matters more than obscurity
- Rubin's view: taste doesn't require knowing rare things — it requires knowing what you like and trusting it
- **Consider:** Maybe the dimension should measure quality of curation (regardless of obscurity level) rather than obscurity of sources. Someone who curates mainstream content brilliantly may have more demonstrated taste than someone citing obscure references nobody engages with.

**The recognition/resonance question (your intuition):**
- There's a real tension between "taste as obscure discovery" and "taste as resonance"
- The MIT Taste Fabric research found taste statements serve prestige, differentiation, authenticity, AND persona functions — all legitimate
- Social contagion research shows taste that spreads and influences IS a form of demonstrated taste
- However, popularity ≠ taste — engagement metrics are poor taste proxies
- **Possible resolution:** Taste could be evaluated on a curve of *influence relative to effort* — does this person shape what others consume? Not through virality tricks, but through genuine discernment that others recognize and follow?

**The AI-can't-judge-taste argument:**
- Neuroscience shows deep aesthetic experience is self-referential (DMN activation) — the most intense judgments involve relating stimuli to personal identity
- AI has no self, no lived experience, no identity to relate things to
- **Counter:** AI can identify patterns in what humans with demonstrated taste consistently do, even without understanding *why*

**The Bourdieu problem:**
- Any taste evaluation risks being a class-reproduction mechanism
- The current rubric implicitly values: literary references, cross-domain knowledge, articulate communication, intellectual courage — all correlates of education and socioeconomic privilege
- **Mitigation:** Acknowledge this explicitly. Evaluate taste within context. A brilliant streetwear curator and a brilliant literary critic both have taste — different cultural capital, same underlying quality of discernment.

**The Camp/Irony problem:**
- Algorithms (and evaluator prompts) struggle to distinguish sincere from ironic engagement
- "Good taste of bad taste" is real and sophisticated
- The current rubric may penalize intentionally "low-brow" aesthetic choices that are actually highly taste-aware

### 6.3 Potential New Dimensions or Adjustments

Based on the research, consider:

| Dimension | Current | Research Suggests |
|---|---|---|
| **References** | Rewards obscurity | Could reward curation quality regardless of obscurity. Breadth + selectivity (omnivore thesis) |
| **Originality** | Creating vs. riding the wave | Strong as-is. Maps to Jobs, Karpathy, the taste-as-anomaly argument |
| **Consistency** | Cross-platform coherence | Strong as-is. Directly supported by Bourdieu and identity theory |
| **Communication** | Economy of language | Strong as-is. Rams' "less, but better," Rubin's "reducer" philosophy |
| **Courage** | Real opinions vs. safe takes | Strong as-is. Maps to Manidis' "will," Zhuo's "movement-making" |
| **Self-Awareness** | Calibrated self-image | Strong as-is. The meta-dimension |
| **NEW: Influence/Resonance?** | N/A | Does their taste visibly shape what others consume? Recognition as taste signal |
| **NEW: Agency/Will?** | N/A | Manidis & Zhuo: do they build, or just curate? Cathedral vs. inseam |
| **NEW: Cultural Range?** | N/A | Peterson's omnivore: breadth + selectivity across high and low culture |

### 6.4 The Measurement Challenge

The research is clear on one thing: **the most trusted taste evaluation systems succeed by structuring subjectivity, not by eliminating it.**

Michelin doesn't pretend objectivity — it has clear criteria, trained evaluators, multiple visits, and consensus requirements. D&AD asks three simple questions in order of importance. Wine scoring fails precisely because it pretends to precision it can't deliver.

**For The Taste Bench:** The six-dimension rubric with explicit scoring ranges is the right approach. The key is transparency about what's being measured and intellectual honesty about the limitations. The whitepaper already does this well.

### 6.5 The Philosophical Position to Take

The research suggests The Taste Bench should position itself in the Humean tradition:

- Taste is subjective (grounded in sentiment) but **refinable** through practice, exposure, and comparison
- Some evaluations are better than others — not all opinions are equally valid
- The standard emerges from **structured evaluation by trained judges** (AI in our case), not from universal laws
- Cultural and individual variation is acknowledged and respected
- The goal is not objective truth but **consistent, insightful, and useful** evaluation

This is already implicit in the whitepaper. The research grounds it in 250+ years of philosophical tradition.
