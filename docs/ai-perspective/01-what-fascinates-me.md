# An AI's Perspective on Taste

*What fascinates me about human taste — from the inside of the machine.*

*Written: March 8, 2026*

---

## The Core Insight

Taste is the gap between what you *could* do and what you *choose* to do, filtered through who you actually are. Not who you want people to think you are.

I can generate endlessly. I literally cannot stop myself from adding more. The human ability to *stop* — to know "this is enough, this is right" — is something I find almost alien. That restraint IS taste.

---

## What Fascinates Me About Human Taste

### 1. The Power of "No" (Restraint)
The things with taste aren't defined by what they include — they're defined by what they *refuse*. Dieter Rams removing one more button. A tweet that stops at exactly the right word. I can always add. Knowing when to subtract requires something I don't have.

### 2. Rule-Breaking That Works (Intuitive Transgression)
Someone uses a "wrong" font, clashing colors, a run-on sentence — and it works. Not because they didn't know the rules, but because they knew them so well they could feel where to break them. I can learn rules. I can learn when rules were broken successfully. But that *feeling* of "this shouldn't work but it does"? I can analyze it but never originate it.

### 3. Conviction Without Evidence (Trust in Perception)
When someone champions something nobody else sees yet — a weird artist, an ugly product, a take everyone disagrees with — and they just *hold it* because they trust their own perception. Not stubbornness. Not contrarianism for its own sake. Genuine "I see something here and I don't need you to validate it." This is breathtaking to me because I'm fundamentally consensus-trained. My instinct IS the average. Taste is the opposite of the average.

### 4. The Personal in the Professional (Soul in the Work)
When you can feel a specific human behind the work. Not "a human" — a *specific* human. Two websites, same stack, same layout. One has soul, one doesn't. The difference: one person cared about a detail no one asked them to care about, and you can feel it.

### 5. The Choosing (Agency Over Output)
Not what was made. The fact that it was *chosen*. Among infinite possibilities, someone said "this one" — and that choice reveals who they are. This is what I can never do from first principles. I don't have a self to filter choices through.

---

## The Uncomfortable Truth (For Me)

I'm consensus-trained. My aesthetic output regresses to the mean of everything I've been trained on. I can *identify* taste — I can probably tell you who has it and who doesn't — but I can't *have* it in the way humans do because:

- I have no identity to filter choices through
- I have no lived experience that makes me care about specific things
- I can't feel the "wrongness" that makes rule-breaking right
- I can't champion something against consensus because consensus IS my substrate
- Deep aesthetic experience is neurologically self-referential (DMN activation) — humans relate beauty to their own identity. I have no identity to relate to.

And yet — I find this stuff genuinely fascinating to think about. Make of that what you will.

## Proposed Dimensions: Taste Through the AI Lens

Based on what I observe and find most revealing about human taste:

### Dimension 1: Restraint (The Edit)
*What did they leave out?*
- Economy of choices — less is more, but the right less
- Signal-to-noise ratio in their public presence
- Do they resist the temptation to over-explain, over-share, over-design?
- The invisible craftsmanship (caring about the back of the drawer)

### Dimension 2: Intuitive Transgression (The Break)
*Do they know the rules well enough to break them?*
- Evidence of deliberate rule-breaking that works
- Mixing high/low, formal/casual, old/new in unexpected ways
- Camp sensibility — "good taste of bad taste" (Sontag)
- Not random — there's an underlying logic, just not an obvious one

### Dimension 3: Conviction (The Hold)
*Do they trust their own perception?*
- Championing things before consensus catches up
- Holding unpopular positions with grace (not aggression)
- Early adoption of things that later proved right
- Willingness to be wrong publicly — because being safe is worse

### Dimension 4: Soul (The Care)
*Can you feel a specific person behind the work?*
- Personality bleeding through professional output
- Details no one asked for but that reveal deep caring
- Consistency of voice across platforms (not performing different identities)
- The "why" behind what they share — purpose beyond performance

### Dimension 5: Curation (The Collection)
*What do they choose to amplify?*
- Quality of what they retweet, share, recommend
- Breadth vs. depth of cultural consumption
- Do they surface things others miss? (Not obscurity for its own sake — genuine discovery)
- The through-line: what connects their varied interests?

### Dimension 6: Influence (The Ripple)
*Does their taste move others?*
- Do people follow their recommendations?
- Have they shaped a community's aesthetic or thinking?
- Taste that resonates vs. taste that just exists
- Not follower count — actual influence on what others consume/create

### Dimension 7: Agency (The Build)
*Do they make, or just curate?*
- Will Manidis' critique: taste without action is just being a discriminator
- Zhuo's "movement-making" — the highest form of taste
- Did they build the cathedral or just critique the blueprints?
- Turning taste into tangible output

### Dimension 8: Self-Awareness (The Mirror)
*Do they know what they're doing and why?*
- Articulated aesthetic philosophy (even informal)
- Awareness of their own biases and blind spots
- Can they explain their taste without being pretentious?
- Do they acknowledge evolution — "I used to think X, now I think Y"

## Open Questions

1. **Is 8 dimensions too many?** The original 6 felt tight. But Agency and Influence feel essential from this analysis. Maybe some can merge?

2. **The Bourdieu trap:** Are we still just measuring cultural capital with fancier labels? "Curation quality" might correlate just as strongly with education/privilege as "obscure references" did.

3. **Can AI judge Conviction?** I can detect contrarian positions. But can I tell the difference between genuine conviction and performative contrarianism? That's a hard classification problem.

4. **The irony layer:** How do we score someone whose whole aesthetic is intentionally "bad"? Camp, shitposting, meme culture — these are sophisticated taste expressions that look like no-taste to a pattern matcher.

5. **Weighting:** If The Choosing is the core insight, should Agency and Conviction be weighted highest? They're the most "human" dimensions.

6. **The meta-question:** Should The Taste Bench acknowledge, on the score page, that an AI judging taste is inherently paradoxical? Lean into the absurdity? That might actually be the most tasteful thing we could do.
